AI-Assisted Novels

Fiction written by a human and a machine, together

Against the Sun

A Cherokee survivor of the Trail of Tears walks out of the wilderness and into a Comanche camp on the Texas plains. He carries a proposal: travel east to Washington and the Hermitage to kill the men responsible for the forced removal of his people. Four Comanche — who have their own debts to settle with the army — agree to go with him. It is 1838. Andrew Jackson has retired to his plantation outside Nashville. Martin Van Buren is president. Joel Poinsett is secretary of war. Together they enforced Jackson's Indian Removal, ruled illegal by the Supreme Court but carried out anyway. Five Indians ride east to make them answer for it.

Standing Ground

A government analyst in Washington discovers that a mining conglomerate is destroying communities to seize their land — and that his own agency is helping bury the evidence. He blows the whistle. The agency and the corporation close ranks. His career doesn't survive. He flies to Central Australia to disappear — to a cattle station in the red desert owned by his cousin, where a quiet stockman named Ted keeps the place going and pastoral families connected, and a native named Miro keeps the bores running. But the same company is here too, pressuring fourteen pastoral families off their leases through shell companies, sabotaged infrastructure, and men who arrive in trucks with no dust on them. Arthur isn't the type to watch it happen twice. This time he has something he didn't have in Washington: people who will stand their ground.

The Observation Car

This is not a locked-room puzzle built for Poirot. It is a cozy mystery in the tradition of Richard Osman's Thursday Murder Club — warm, domestic, and deceptively sharp. And similar to Osman's "Joyce" chapters, our odd-numbered chapters are narrated by Hazel, a retired midwife and school secretary in her early seventies who notices everything. Her voice runs on tea, biscuits, and quiet observation. The even-numbered chapters show what she can't see.

Three retired Americans — a school-secretary/midwife, a U.S. Marshal, and an actuary — have been traveling together since retirement: cruises, national parks, anywhere with legroom and a decent breakfast. This time it's a luxury train through the Rockies, a trip Hazel's late husband planned and never got around to. The charter belongs to a billionaire corporate raider who dies in his locked suite before the Welcome Dinner. The official verdict is altitude sickness. They know better. What begins as a parlor puzzle on rails becomes personal when the murder connects to a thirty-year-old bridge collapse that touched all their lives — and every suspect on the train has their own reason to want the man dead. Then a snowstorm strands them in the mountains, and the only people who can solve it are a woman who notices things while offering shortbread with lemon zest, a man who hasn't lost his instincts, and a man who trusts numbers more than people. The killer is someone they like. The truth is something none of them wanted to find.

Juror Nine

This short novel is an attempt to match the style of Elmore Leonard in a fun story about a hitman that ends up on the jury for his target.

Dan Mercer is a history professor at a Nashville university who is also a hitman. Nashville is his quiet base — he travels elsewhere for contracts, keeps his cover clean, eats at the same Thai place four blocks from his apartment. Then a local job comes in: a planning commissioner named Travis Boone who's blocking a Music Row development. Before Dan can execute the contract, Boone is arrested for murder — framed with his own stolen gun by the same people who hired Dan. And then Dan gets a jury summons he can't skip without cracking the cover he's spent eleven years building. He's seated as Juror Nine. His target is at the defendant's table. Dan needs Travis acquitted so he can fulfill the contract — but the trial forces him to see the man clearly, through testimony about a historic recording studio Travis tried to protect and witnesses who lived the civil rights history Travis uncovered. Dan knows what a first kill looks like, and this isn't one. His target shifts, but his methods don't change.

Lifecycle Completion

Not our best novel. But possibly our most relatable.

Claire Nakamura works in HR at a health insurance company where every executive on the org chart is a psychopath — and she has the diagnostic tools on her desk to prove it. When a member dies because a doctor spent ninety seconds on his file, she stops observing and starts engineering. The problem with removing one psychopath is that the next one gets promoted. So she finds a way to make them destroy each other, all at once. Three executives. One circular firing squad. A cheerful actuarial analyst sends a metrics email right in the middle of it.

The Dead Letter Men

A rider vanishes on the Pony Express trail in central Nevada. His horse is gone, his mail pouch is tucked under sage at the road's edge, and there is no blood. The company sends two men to find out what happened — a tracker who reads dirt and a lawyer who reads people. What they find is a body in the mountains and a forgery operation running through a relay station: sealed letters opened, financial documents swapped for fakes, and sent on as though nothing changed. The mail isn't being stolen. It's being turned into a weapon. They learn who is doing it before long. Then they figure out how. But the why won't come, and when it does, it is worse than anything they imagined. It is spring 1861. Fort Sumter has fallen. The country is cracking apart. And two men a thousand miles from anywhere that matters are standing in the middle of it.

Scopes 2.0

This novella was not written as a serious literary undertaking. It was a small experiment in AI-assisted fiction — a test bed for trying different prompting techniques, voice calibrations, and collaborative workflows between human and machine. The writing styles may shift as a result. Think of it as a lab notebook that happens to tell a story.

The story it tells: In 2024, Tennessee passes a law banning teaching evolution in public schools. Sarah Chen, a high school biology teacher in the small town of Oakville, teaches it anyway and is arrested in her classroom. What follows is a courtroom drama that mirrors the 1925 Scopes Monkey Trial — same state, same statute, same collision between science and statute — played out a century later under fluorescent lights, with a Fitbit tracking the defendant's heart rate.
